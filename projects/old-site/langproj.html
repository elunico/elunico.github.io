Constructed Languages (or conlangs) are languages that are created purposely by individuals or a group, rather than evolved naturally over time by human communication. There are many reasons why people conlang including but not limited to fantasy world-creation for games or story telling, giving people a common neutral language for international communication, linguistic exercises or challenges that involve making languages with certain constraints, secret codes, linguistic experiments or sometimes just for fun. Regardless, of why a person or group creates a language, a lot goes into it, and it can be a surprisingly rich reflection of the people who made it and the context surrounding its creation.

I have been fascinated by language for many years and minored in linguistics, and for me, creating languages has always been a great way to explore various linguistic principles and phenomena. When creating a language, one can choose to start from nothing, building up a language from its most fundamental words and elements, perhaps simulating evolution over hundreds of years according to known linguistic principles. They can also start with a language or family that exists and imagine how small variations or dialects might play out over time. While I have taken both approaches, I tend to prefer the latter. Taking Latin as my base, I have created a number of hypothetical Romance languages among other kinds of languages as well.

Many of the languages that I have created are at varying levels of completion. This is because, typically, if I find a language is not working in practice as well as it is in theory I will choose to change things up, but I usually leave the language where it is and start again on the same base but without whatever the problematic feature is. Furthermore, it is hard to say when any language is complete. Conlangs, like natural languages, can evolve either by natural spoken processes (a good example of this is El Glheth Talossa by R. Ben Madison) or it can "evolve" purposely by the creator defining evolutionary rules.

Llengua Provitana

Llengua Provitana is a constructed Romance language and one of the most recent languages I created. It is based on the languages around Catalonia in Spain and has a heavy resemblance to Catalan. It differs in some key ways and includes some "regional variation" between two fictitious regions of east and west. The eastern dialects tend to contain more Spanish-like features and western dialects tend to contain more French-like features, as if the language was spoken in modern day Catalonia.

This language was very fun to create. Being later in my conlang jouney, it was a more familiar process. I was inspired to create this after leanring more about the languages of Western Spain. I really enjoy Catalan and I think this might be the language most similar to an existing language that I have ever created.

Provitan exists as an article on The Conlang Wiki where a description of the language can be found.

Tôdos los essers humans són naizuts libres et iquals en dignità e dêstros e són dotat con râçón e conciència e deven comportarse a unos a los altros nel espírit de la fraternità.

Lingua Lațiense

Lingua Lațiense is another constructed Romance language (I have something of a thing for Romance languages) that is a little older than Provitan. Latiense is fairly well developed and unlike the other Romance languages I created, it is an eastern Romance language. Specifically it is a language that lies east of La Spezia–Rimini Line. In Romance linguistics, this is a line along the northern part of Italy that separates two broad families of Romance languages: Eastern Ones and Western Ones, and there are several characteristics that belong to each family that tend not to cross that line. (I am simplifying a little bit here) such as pronunciation of soft 'c' and the mechanism behind pluralization of nouns.

Latiense is unlike most Romance languages in that it has a noun declension system. Nouns in Latiense change endings not only for singular and plural as they do in Spanish, English, and many other languages, but also for their role in the sentence. You would expect the word 'child' in the sentences 'I saw the child' and 'The child saw me' to have different endings in Latiense, not unlike how the word 'I' in English takes different forms. The only major Romance language to feature this is Romanian, so this was a fun aspect of the language to explore. Like Romanian, and unlike many modern Romance languages, however, Latin does have an actually quite extensive case system for nouns, so its not so much that Romanian or indeed Latiense is innovative as much as they are less willing to part with features of Latin.

Latiense does not currently exist anywhere on the Internet as Provitan does. This is due to it being a little older and not as well organized in its documentation as Provitan.

Toti i essi humani son nașen libri et iquali in dignitatis e destror. Ei son donati de ratzonis e conșienzias e deven agí l’uno verse lor altror nel spírito fraternitatis.

Tilõ Niqacaw

Tilõ Niqacaw is a conlang that is not based on any other language. That is not to say that it uses features that are unheard of in any language. In fact it uses sound and grammar features exclusively found in natural human languages. However, the choice of which features are used and their combinations as well as all the sounds that are present, and the vocabulary that it uses are all made up from scratch with little influence from existing languages.

Tilo is the most developed a priori conlang that I have made and it is also the most recent. It is also one of my favorites as the philosophy and design behind it, to me, it the best and nicest looking. That being said, it is still fairly behind the development of the Romance languages. This is due to the sort of "template" that Romance languages provide. Creating a conlang of a Romance language can be quite restrictive, because there are many sources of features that can be drawn from. In the case of something like Tilo, everything has to be chosen and decided on, so its a much slower process. None of the a priori languages I have created are as far along in development since they require so much building. Even when the grammar and the sounds of the language are laid out, it remains nearly unusable until sufficient amounts of vocabulary are created from scratch. Since Tilo, like all of my conlangs, aim to sound natural, though very foreign, to humans, it is necessary that the vocabulary be self-consistent and make sense.

All this being said, there is a page on the Conlang Wiki where you can find some information on Tilõ Niqacaw and how it works so far
